The Problem

Logging in and signing up can feel tedious and stringent since they often contain many choices, questions, and requirements. Although these additional features strengthen security and privacy, they can also leave users feeling anxious and overwhelmed. How can we improve the current login and signup experience to better serve and support students?

Research and Analysis

To gather a clear idea of my project concept and who its users might be, I created an initial empathy map for my ideal users. I tested my empathy map by interviewing a group of users who fit within my target demographic to understand pain and gain points that my project may need to address. From these interviews, I built user personas and broke down a list of general traits and user journeys to illustrate who my target market is.

From these interviews, I concluded three consistent issues students faced while logging in and signing up:

  1. Students want a quick login and signup process that involves a limited number of steps.
  2. Students want a simple account and password recovery process without needing to depend on security questions or substantial user authentications.
  3. After inputting their information, students want to be instantly informed of potential errors to save time and effort.

Site Map

After breaking down the login and signup process, I began outlining its initial scope and information architecture to create its sitemap. Since several users stressed that current login and signup pages contain numerous steps, my goal was to minimize the number of actions it took to log in and sign up to avoid a tedious flow. This approach allowed me to identify critical steps, features, and potential edge cases users would encounter.

Wireframing

During this phase, I updated my site map based on past critiques and testing and began integrating the user flow to create initial wireframes. I focused on designing 6-10 core pages with minimal content to allow me to visualize and delineate content concepts and layouts.

Low-Fidelity Test

I began field testing my wireframes with the 2nd set of users that fit within my target market to explore their user journeys, preferences, and goals. Introducing these initial layout concepts allowed me to gauge my target market's first impressions and suggestions for improvement. Based on the user feedback I received, I updated my wireframes and compiled the remaining of my responsive pages.

Mid-Fidelity Test

After integrating the branding and visuals, I reinterviewed past users to test the usability of my designs. I divided my interviews into three parts:

  1. Pre-testing for delving into mental models.
  2. In-testing in which I asked users to complete four tasks (creating an account, logging into an account, and recovering their username and password) to understand and explore their experience with the prototype.
  3. Post-testing for collecting feedback about layout designs, pages, and other visual elements.

I then outlined a list of possible edge cases they may encounter and designed a responsive page for each:

  1. Creating an account
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username is already taken.
    3. Case 3: Error, the username contains an unrecognizable character.
    4. Case 4: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    5. Case 5: Error, the password length is too short or long.
    6. Case 6: Error, the email address format is incorrect.
    7. Case 7: Error, the email address is already registered.
  2. Logging into an account
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    3. Case 3: Error, the password length is too short.
    4. Case 4: Error, the username and password meet requirements but do not match records.
  3. Recovering their username
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the email address format is incorrect.
  4. Recovering their username
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    3. Case 3: Error, the email address format is incorrect.

Overall, users were able to navigate through and pass every task on their first attempt. Users stated that the procedures for creating and logging into an account and recovering their username and password were intuitive, clear, and straightforward. By conducting my interviews early on, I managed to reveal any need for design updates ahead of time and implemented these changes accordingly.

Result

A demonstration of the login and registration process for Pomolo.
