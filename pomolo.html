The Problem

Students claim that logging in and signing up can feel tedious and stringent since it often contains many choices, questions, and requirements. Although these additional features strengthen security and privacy, it can also leave students feeling anxious and overwhelmed. Taking these pain points and frustrations into consideration, I wondered, how can I improve the current login and signup experience to better serve and support students?

The Solution

Pomolo, a time management system app that encourages students to work with the time they have, offers a multifunctional onboarding process that's compatible on mobile, tablet, and desktop platforms. Pomolo only asks for the necessities to deliver a signup and login process that's smooth, quick, and to the point. With Pomolo, my goal is to relieve students, not overhwlem them.

Research and Analysis

To gather a clear idea of my project concept, I began by research by studying top competitors, trends, and the target market for focus tracking apps. Once I collected the initial research data, I then created an empathy map to field test my concepts with potential users. Next, I interviewed a group of users that fit within my target demographic and gathered pain and gain points to convey any issues my project may need to address. From these interviews, I built user personas and journeys, and broke down a list of general traits to illustrate the target market.

Based on my interviews, I concluded three consistent changes students hope to see in the registration and login process:

  1. Students want a quick login and signup process that involves a limited number of steps.
  2. Students want a simple account and password recovery process without needing to depend on security questions or substantial user authentications.
  3. Students want to be instantly informed of potential errors to save time and effort while inputting their information.

Site Map

After breaking down the login and signup process, I began outlining the initial scope and information architecture to create a sitemap. Since several users stressed the monotonousness of current onboarding processes, my goal was to minimize the number of steps it took to log in and sign up to avoid a tedious flow. This approach allowed me to identify critical features, strategies, and potential edge cases that would be essential to the registration and login experience.

Wireframing

During this phase, I updated my site map based on critiques I gathered from past testing. I then integrated the user flow to create initial wireframes and focused on designing its core pages to visualize and delineate content concepts and layouts.

Low-Fidelity Test

I began field testing my wireframes with the 2nd set of users that fit within my target market to explore their user journeys, preferences, and goals. Introducing these initial layout concepts allowed me to gauge my target market's first impressions and suggestions for improvement. Based on the user feedback I received, I updated my wireframes and compiled the remaining of my responsive pages.

Mid-Fidelity Test

After integrating the branding and visuals, I reinterviewed past users to test the usability of my designs. I divided my interviews into three parts:

  1. Pre-testing for delving into mental models.
  2. In-testing in which I asked users to complete four tasks (creating an account, logging into an account, and recovering their username and password) to understand and explore their experience with the prototype.
  3. Post-testing for collecting feedback about layout designs, pages, and other visual elements.

I then outlined a list of possible edge cases they may encounter and designed a responsive page for each:

  1. Creating an account
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username is already taken.
    3. Case 3: Error, the username contains an unrecognizable character.
    4. Case 4: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    5. Case 5: Error, the password length is too short or long.
    6. Case 6: Error, the email address format is incorrect.
    7. Case 7: Error, the email address is already registered.
  2. Logging into an account
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    3. Case 3: Error, the password length is too short.
    4. Case 4: Error, the username and password meet requirements but do not match records.
  3. Recovering their username
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the email address format is incorrect.
  4. Recovering their username
    1. Case 1: Success, the inputted information meets all requirements and is approved.
    2. Case 2: Error, the username length is too short or long.
    3. Case 3: Error, the email address format is incorrect.

Overall, users were able to navigate through and pass every task on their first attempt. Users stated that the procedures for creating and logging into an account and recovering their username and password were intuitive, clear, and straightforward. By conducting my interviews early on, I managed to reveal any need for design updates ahead of time and implemented these changes accordingly.

Result

A demonstration of the login and registration process for Pomolo.

 
Limited format requirements during registration

Hassle-free sign-ups requiring no special characters, capitalized letters, or numbers. Users have the freedom to customize their username and password so long as usernames contain 3-15 symbol-free characters and passwords contain 8 characters or more.

Instant notification of errors

If users input information that's incorrectly formatted or does not meet guidelines, they will be immediately notified to save multiple login attempts and effort.

 
Touch-ID and Face-ID

Users have the option to conveniently access their account with fingerprint and facial authentication technology. Instead of typing out each field to log in, users can quickly and securely verify their identity with a touch of a fingerprint or facial scan.

Reflection

When I began this project 3 months ago, I thought I knew all there was to a successful login and signup process. Little did I know I would embark on a journey that would require extensive user research, iterations, and testing to produce a product with intentional designs and solve recognized issues. Enthralled by the opportunity to define the onboarding process, I began a journey of analyzing different registration flows and iteratively transformed my project to provide the smoothest signup and login process.

As I acquired further insight from my user research, I consistently reexamined my prototypes to make critical changes to satisfy pain points and meet users' needs. I learned so much about how to build a successful product from start to finish, understanding user motivations, usability testing and analysis, and designing a product for a specific intention. Altogether this was an incredibly rewarding experience and it could not have been possible without the guidance and support of my professor, Professor Hawk.
